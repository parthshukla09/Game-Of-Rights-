Articles of the Constitution of India

Article 12: Definition of the State

  1. Purpose:

    Article 12 defines the term "State" specifically for the purposes of Part III of the Constitution, which deals with Fundamental Rights.

  2. Entities Included:
    • Government of India: This includes the central government and all its departments and ministries.
    • Government and Legislature of Each State: This refers to the governments and legislative bodies of all Indian states, including both the executive and legislative branches.
    • Local Authorities: This encompasses various local government bodies such as municipalities, panchayats, and municipal corporations that function at the local level.
    • Other Authorities: This includes any other bodies or authorities that are empowered by the Constitution or laws of India to perform functions of a governmental nature.
  3. Significance:
    • Enforcement of Fundamental Rights: By defining "State" broadly, Article 12 ensures that Fundamental Rights are enforceable not just against the central and state governments, but also against various local authorities and other bodies performing government functions.
    • Judicial Review: Courts can issue writs and enforce Fundamental Rights against these entities if they violate any of the rights guaranteed under Part III of the Constitution.
  4. Exclusion:

    Private Bodies: Article 12 does not cover private organizations or individuals. Fundamental Rights are enforceable against the State but not directly against private entities unless they are performing public or governmental functions.

  5. Interpretation:

    The definition of "State" under Article 12 has been interpreted by the judiciary to include various authorities and bodies involved in the governance process, ensuring comprehensive protection of Fundamental Rights.

Article 13: Laws Inconsistent with or in Derogation of Fundamental Rights

  1. Inconsistency with Fundamental Rights:
    • Article 13 states that any law or ordinance that is inconsistent with or in derogation of Fundamental Rights is void.
    • This means that any law that contravenes the Fundamental Rights enshrined in Part III of the Constitution is not valid and cannot be enforced.
  2. Definition of Law:

    The term 'law' includes:

    • Acts of Parliament or State Legislatures: Formal laws passed by the legislative bodies.
    • Ordinances: Temporary laws enacted by the President or Governors.
    • Rules, Regulations, Orders: Made by the executive authorities under statutory powers.
  3. Judicial Review:
    • The Judiciary has the power to review laws and determine their validity in light of Fundamental Rights.
    • Courts can declare laws unconstitutional if they violate Fundamental Rights.
  4. Exceptions:

    Article 13(3) provides that laws that were in force before the commencement of the Constitution and are inconsistent with Fundamental Rights are not automatically void, but can be challenged.

  5. Retrospective Effect:

    The article does not apply retrospectively to laws that existed before the Constitution came into effect but provides for a review of new laws in light of Fundamental Rights.

Article 14: Right to Equality

  1. Equality Before Law:
    • Article 14 guarantees that all persons, within the territory of India, are equal before the law.
    • This means that the law will treat every individual equally, without any discrimination.
  2. Equal Protection of the Laws:
    • Article 14 also ensures equal protection of the laws, meaning that individuals in similar situations must be treated similarly.
    • This principle aims to prevent arbitrary discrimination by the state.
  3. Prohibition of Arbitrary Actions:
    • Article 14 prohibits any form of arbitrary or discriminatory state action.
    • The state must justify any classification it makes between different groups of people as reasonable and not arbitrary.
  4. Reasonable Classification:
    • While Article 14 ensures equality, it allows for reasonable classification. This means that the state can create different laws for different groups if there is a legitimate purpose and a rational basis for doing so.
    • The classification must be based on an intelligible differentia and have a rational nexus to the objective sought to be achieved.
  5. Universal Application:

    The principles of Article 14 apply universally to all individuals, whether citizens or non-citizens, within the territory of India.

Article 15: Prohibition of Discrimination on Grounds of Religion, Race, Caste, Sex, or Place of Birth

  1. No Discrimination by the State:
    • Article 15 prohibits the state from discriminating against any citizen on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth.
    • This ensures that all citizens have equal access to public resources and services.
  2. Access to Public Places:

    Article 15(2) states that no citizen shall be denied access to shops, public restaurants, hotels, and places of public entertainment or the use of wells, tanks, bathing ghats, roads, and places of public resort maintained wholly or partly out of state funds.

  3. Special Provisions for Women and Children:

    Article 15(3) allows the state to make special provisions for women and children, recognizing the need for affirmative action to uplift these groups.

  4. Advancement of Socially and Educationally Backward Classes:

    Article 15(4) permits the state to make special provisions for the advancement of any socially and educationally backward classes of citizens, as well as for Scheduled Castes (SCs) and Scheduled Tribes (STs).

  5. Reservation in Educational Institutions:

    Article 15(5) empowers the state to make special provisions for the reservation of seats in educational institutions, including private institutions, for SCs, STs, and other backward classes (OBCs), except minority educational institutions.

  6. Exception for Religious and Minority Institutions:

    Article 15(6) allows the state to provide reservation in educational institutions for economically weaker sections of society.

Article 16: Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment

  1. Equal Opportunity:

    Article 16 guarantees equality of opportunity for all citizens in matters relating to employment or appointment to any office under the State.

  2. No Discrimination in Public Employment:

    Article 16(2) prohibits discrimination in public employment on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, descent, place of birth, or residence.

  3. Residence Requirement:

    Article 16(3) allows Parliament to make laws requiring a residence within a state as a condition for public employment or appointment.

  4. Reservation for Backward Classes:

    Article 16(4) enables the State to reserve appointments or posts for backward classes of citizens if they are not adequately represented in public services.

  5. Reservation in Promotions:

    Article 16(4A) allows the State to make provisions for the reservation of promotions for SCs and STs if they are not adequately represented in public services.

  6. Carry Forward of Reserved Vacancies:

    Article 16(4B) allows for the carry-forward of unfilled reserved vacancies to subsequent years, ensuring that reservations are met even if vacancies are not filled in a particular year.

  7. Religious Institutions:

    Article 16(5) permits laws requiring officeholders in religious or denominational institutions to belong to a particular religion or denomination.

Article 17: Abolition of Untouchability

  1. Abolition of Untouchability:
    • Article 17 abolishes untouchability and forbids its practice in any form.
    • It declares that untouchability, which is a social practice of discrimination and segregation based on caste, is illegal and unacceptable.
  2. Prohibition by the State:
    • The State is prohibited from enforcing or recognizing untouchability in any form.
    • This includes any acts or practices that discriminate against individuals based on caste.
  3. Criminal Offense:
    • Any practice of untouchability is deemed a criminal offense.
    • The law provides for penalties and punishments for those who practice or perpetuate untouchability.
  4. Scope and Application:
    • The prohibition of untouchability applies to all public places and institutions.
    • It encompasses all forms of segregation, exclusion, and discrimination based on caste.
  5. Legal Measures:
    • The Constitution empowers the State to take legislative and administrative measures to eradicate untouchability.
    • The law ensures that those affected by untouchability practices have legal recourse and protection.
  6. Social Reform:

    Article 17 reflects the commitment to social justice and equality. It is a significant step towards eliminating caste-based discrimination and promoting social integration.

Article 18: Abolition of Titles

  1. Prohibition of Titles:

    Article 18 abolishes titles except for military and academic distinctions. It prohibits the state from conferring any titles on citizens, thereby promoting equality.

  2. International Titles:

    Citizens of India are also prohibited from accepting any title from a foreign state without the consent of the President.

  3. Abolition of Titles of Nobility:

    The article also abolishes titles related to the feudal system, such as "Raja," "Maharaja," "Nawab," and similar titles that imply aristocracy or privileged status.

  4. Encouragement of Equality:

    By prohibiting titles and honors, Article 18 promotes the principle of equality and discourages the creation of distinctions based on status or rank.

Article 19: Protection of Certain Rights Regarding Freedom of Speech, etc.

  1. Freedom of Speech and Expression:

    Guarantees the right to freedom of speech and expression (Article 19(1)(a)).

  2. Freedom of Assembly:

    Protects the right to assemble peaceably and without arms (Article 19(1)(b)).

  3. Freedom of Association:

    Ensures the right to form associations or unions (Article 19(1)(c)).

  4. Freedom of Movement:

    Guarantees the right to move freely throughout the territory of India (Article 19(1)(d)).

  5. Freedom of Residence:

    Protects the right to reside and settle in any part of India (Article 19(1)(e)).

  6. Right to Practice any Profession:

    Ensures the right to practice any profession, trade, or business (Article 19(1)(g)).

  7. Restrictions:

    The rights under Article 19 are subject to reasonable restrictions imposed by the State in the interests of the sovereignty and integrity of India, the security of the State, friendly relations with foreign states, public order, decency, morality, or in relation to contempt of court, defamation, or incitement to an offense (Article 19(2) to (6)).

Article 20: Protection in Respect of Conviction for Offenses

  1. Protection Against Ex Post Facto Laws:

    Article 20(1) prohibits the enactment of laws that penalize an act that was not an offense at the time it was committed (ex post facto laws).

  2. Protection Against Double Jeopardy:

    Article 20(2) protects individuals from being tried or punished more than once for the same offense (double jeopardy).

  3. Protection Against Self-Incrimination:

    Article 20(3) guarantees that no person accused of an offense shall be compelled to be a witness against themselves (protection against self-incrimination).

  4. Non-Retroactive Effect:

    The article ensures that no one is punished under laws that are retroactive, and no one is forced to testify against themselves in criminal cases.

Article 21: Protection of Life and Personal Liberty

  1. Right to Life and Personal Liberty:

    Article 21 guarantees that no person shall be deprived of their life or personal liberty except according to the procedure established by law.

  2. Broad Interpretation:

    The scope of Article 21 has been interpreted broadly by the Supreme Court to include various rights essential to the enjoyment of life and personal liberty.

  3. Right to a Fair Trial:

    The right to a fair trial and legal representation is an integral part of Article 21, ensuring that individuals facing criminal charges have access to a just and fair legal process.

  4. Judicial Activism:

    The judiciary has played an active role in interpreting Article 21 to include rights such as the right to a clean environment, right to education, and right to health care, emphasizing its role in safeguarding a broad spectrum of fundamental rights.

  5. Protection Against Arbitrary Detention:

    Article 21 provides protection against arbitrary arrest and detention, ensuring that any deprivation of liberty is carried out under a lawful process and with due regard to legal safeguards.

Article 22: Protection Against Arrest and Detention

  1. Rights of Persons Arrested:

    Article 22 provides specific protections for individuals who are arrested or detained, including the right to be informed of the grounds for arrest and the right to consult a legal practitioner of their choice.

  2. Detention Without Trial:

    It restricts detention without trial to a maximum of three months, unless an advisory board, appointed by the government, confirms the necessity of detention beyond this period (Article 22(4)).

  3. Preventive Detention:

    Preventive detention is allowed under Article 22, but such detention must be based on a law passed by the legislature and must comply with procedural safeguards to prevent misuse.

  4. Right to be Produced Before a Magistrate:

    Article 22(2) mandates that every person arrested must be produced before a magistrate within 24 hours of arrest, excluding the time necessary for the journey from the place of arrest to the court.

  5. Legal Safeguards:

    The article ensures that any detention or arrest is subject to judicial scrutiny and compliance with constitutional safeguards to prevent arbitrary detention.

Article 23: Prohibition of Traffic in Human Beings and Forced Labor

  1. Prohibition of Human Trafficking:

    Article 23 prohibits human trafficking and forced labor, declaring that trafficking in human beings for any purpose is illegal and punishable by law.

  2. Prohibition of Forced Labor:

    It bans forced labor, ensuring that no individual shall be compelled to work against their will, except for duties imposed by law, such as community service or legal obligations.

  3. Exemptions:

    Certain types of labor, such as that imposed as a part of normal civic duties or as punishment for criminal offenses, are exempted from this prohibition.

  4. Legal Enforcement:

    The State is empowered to legislate and enforce laws to prevent and punish human trafficking and forced labor, reflecting a commitment to human rights and dignity.

  5. Judicial Measures:

    The judiciary has been active in interpreting and enforcing Article 23 to address issues related to bonded labor, child labor, and other forms of exploitation.

Article 24: Prohibition of Employment of Children in Factories, etc.

  1. Prohibition of Child Labor:

    Article 24 prohibits the employment of children below the age of fourteen years in factories, mines, or any other hazardous employment.

  2. Focus on Hazardous Occupations:

    The prohibition is particularly focused on employment in hazardous industries where working conditions may be detrimental to the health and development of children.

  3. Legislative Action:

    The State is responsible for enacting laws and regulations to enforce this prohibition and to ensure that children are not engaged in such employment.

  4. Right to Education:

    The prohibition is also linked to the right to education, ensuring that children are provided with opportunities for education rather than being forced into labor.

  5. Protection and Welfare:

    Article 24 underscores the importance of child protection and welfare, emphasizing the need for a safe and conducive environment for children’s growth and development.

Article 25: Freedom of Conscience and Free Profession, Practice, and Propagation of Religion

  1. Freedom of Religion:

    Article 25 guarantees the freedom of conscience and the right to freely profess, practice, and propagate religion.

  2. Public Order, Health, and Morality:

    The freedom to practice religion is subject to reasonable restrictions in the interests of public order, health, and morality (Article 25(1)).

  3. Religious Institutions:

    Article 25(2) allows the State to regulate and control religious institutions and their management to ensure that they do not violate public policies or regulations.

  4. Equality Before Law:

    The article ensures that all religious groups and institutions are subject to the same laws and regulations, preventing any form of discrimination based on religion.

  5. Right to Manage Religious Affairs:

    Individuals and groups have the right to manage their religious affairs, including the administration of religious institutions and their properties, subject to compliance with general laws.

Article 26: Freedom to Manage Religious Affairs

  1. Right to Manage Religious Affairs:

    Article 26 guarantees the right to every religious denomination or any section thereof to manage its own religious affairs.

  2. Autonomy of Religious Groups:

    Religious denominations have the autonomy to manage their own affairs, including the appointment of priests, conduct of religious ceremonies, and administration of properties.

  3. Regulation by Law:

    While the management of religious affairs is largely self-regulated, Article 26 permits the State to regulate or restrict such management if it is necessary to ensure public order, morality, and health.

  4. Charitable Institutions:

    The article extends to the establishment and maintenance of charitable institutions connected with religion, ensuring that they can operate according to their religious practices.

  5. Property Management:

    Religious denominations have the right to own and manage properties, and these properties are protected from arbitrary interference by the State.

Article 27: Freedom from Taxes for Promoting Religion

  1. No Tax for Promotion of Religion:

    Article 27 ensures that no person shall be compelled to pay taxes for the promotion or maintenance of any particular religion.

  2. Secularism in Taxation:

    The provision upholds the principle of secularism by ensuring that taxation does not support or discriminate against any religious group.

  3. Exceptions:

    The article does not prevent the State from using public funds for educational institutions maintained by religious groups if they are open to all, including those from different religions.

Article 28: Freedom from Religious Instruction in Certain Educational Institutions

  1. No Religious Instruction in Government-Funded Schools:

    Article 28 mandates that no religious instruction shall be provided in any educational institution wholly maintained out of State funds.

    This ensures that government-funded schools and institutions remain neutral with respect to religion.

  2. Permissibility in Private Institutions:

    The article allows religious instruction in private educational institutions, provided that attendance is optional and the institution is not funded by the State.

    This means private schools or colleges can impart religious education if they are privately funded.

  3. Religious Education in State-Funded Institutions:

    Even in State-funded institutions, students can be exposed to religion through academic studies, such as history or comparative religion, as long as it does not constitute religious instruction.

  4. Freedom of Choice:

    Students have the right to choose whether or not to participate in religious instruction offered by private institutions, reinforcing individual freedom in educational settings.

Article 29: Protection of Interests of Minorities

  1. Right to Preserve Culture:

    Article 29 provides that any section of citizens with a distinct language, script, or culture has the right to conserve and protect their cultural heritage.

    This ensures that minorities can preserve their cultural identity and traditions.

  2. Right to Establish and Administer Educational Institutions:

    Article 29(1) guarantees the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice, allowing minorities to set up institutions that cater to their community’s educational needs.

  3. Protection Against Discrimination:

    Article 29(2) protects individuals from being denied admission to educational institutions receiving State aid on grounds of religion, race, caste, or language.

    This ensures non-discrimination in educational access and promotes inclusivity.

Article 30: Right of Minorities to Establish and Administer Educational Institutions

  1. Right to Establish Institutions:

    Article 30 guarantees that all religious and linguistic minorities have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice.

    This provision supports the autonomy of minority groups in providing education that reflects their cultural and religious values.

  2. State Aid to Minority Institutions:

    The State is obligated to grant aid to minority institutions on a non-discriminatory basis, ensuring that such institutions receive fair treatment in the allocation of resources.

  3. Protection Against Discrimination:

    The article ensures that minority educational institutions can operate freely and are protected from arbitrary interference or discrimination by the State.

  4. Regulation by Law:

    While minority institutions have the right to manage their own affairs, they are subject to general laws related to standards of education and administration.

Article 31: (Repealed by the 44th Amendment Act, 1978)

  1. Historical Context:

    Article 31 was originally part of the Constitution and dealt with the right to property, specifically providing that no person could be deprived of property except by authority of law.

  2. Abolition:

    The provision was repealed by the 44th Amendment Act, 1978, which removed the fundamental right to property from the list of Fundamental Rights and moved it to the category of legal rights under ordinary law.

Article 32: Remedies for Enforcement of Fundamental Rights

  1. Right to Constitutional Remedies:

    Article 32 guarantees the right to approach the Supreme Court for the enforcement of Fundamental Rights.

    This provision allows individuals to seek redressal for violations of their Fundamental Rights directly from the highest court.

  2. Writ Jurisdiction:

    The Supreme Court has the power to issue writs, including habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto, and certiorari, to enforce Fundamental Rights and ensure justice.

  3. Role of the Supreme Court:

    Article 32 establishes the Supreme Court as the protector and guarantor of Fundamental Rights, emphasizing its role in upholding constitutional rights and providing remedies for violations.

  4. Alternative Remedies:

    While Article 32 provides for direct recourse to the Supreme Court, individuals may also seek remedies through High Courts under Article 226 for enforcement of Fundamental Rights, which are more localized.

Article 33: Power to Modify the Rights in Their Application to Armed Forces, etc.

  1. Modification of Rights:

    Article 33 allows Parliament to modify the application of Fundamental Rights for members of the Armed Forces, paramilitary forces, police forces, or similar organizations.

    This means that the rights may be adjusted to accommodate the special needs and duties of these forces.

  2. Maintenance of Discipline:

    The modification is intended to ensure that the discipline and functioning of these forces are not compromised by the Fundamental Rights provisions.

    It allows for specific regulations and restrictions necessary for maintaining order and efficiency within these organizations.

  3. Legislative Authority:

    The power to modify Fundamental Rights in this context is exercised through legislation passed by Parliament, ensuring that changes are made within a legal framework.

Article 34: Restriction on Rights Conferred by Part III While Martial Law is in Force

  1. Martial Law and Fundamental Rights:

    Article 34 provides that when martial law is in force in any area, the Fundamental Rights under Part III of the Constitution may be restricted or suspended.

    This is applicable in situations where martial law is declared due to a grave emergency, such as armed rebellion or invasion.

  2. Legislative Action:

    Restrictions or suspensions of Fundamental Rights under martial law must be sanctioned by law enacted by Parliament, ensuring that such actions are legally authorized.

  3. Protection of Human Rights:

    Despite the possibility of restrictions, the rights under Article 21 (right to life and personal liberty) remain protected even during martial law, reflecting the essential nature of this right.

Article 35: Powers of Parliament to Make Laws

  1. Legislative Powers:

    Article 35 empowers Parliament to make laws to give effect to the provisions of Fundamental Rights and to specify the penalties for their violation.

    It ensures that legislative measures can be enacted to enforce and implement the rights guaranteed by the Constitution.

  2. Supremacy of Parliament:

    The provision highlights the role of Parliament in establishing laws that define and protect Fundamental Rights, reflecting the legislative authority in safeguarding constitutional rights.

  3. Implementation of Rights:

    The laws enacted under Article 35 help in translating the abstract guarantees of Fundamental Rights into concrete legal provisions and mechanisms for enforcement.
