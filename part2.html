Articles of the Constitution of India - Part 1

Article 5: Citizenship at the Commencement of the Constitution

  • Citizenship for Domiciled Persons:
    • Article 5 grants citizenship to individuals who were domiciled in India at the commencement of the Constitution (January 26, 1950).
    • "Domicile" refers to the place where a person has a permanent home or intends to live indefinitely.
  • Criteria for Citizenship:
    • Citizenship is conferred on a person if they meet any one of the following criteria:
      • Born in India: The individual was born in India.
      • Parentage: Either of the person’s parents was born in India.
      • Residency: The person had been ordinarily resident in India for at least five years immediately preceding the commencement of the Constitution.
  • Broad and Inclusive Framework:
    • Article 5 provided a broad and inclusive definition of citizenship, encompassing various scenarios to ensure that most people residing in India at the time of independence were recognized as citizens.
  • Key Role in Nation-Building:
    • By granting citizenship to a wide range of individuals, Article 5 played a crucial role in the formation of the Indian nation, ensuring stability and continuity in the population as the country transitioned from colonial rule to independence.
  • Foundation of Citizenship Laws:
    • Article 5 forms the foundation of Indian citizenship law, and subsequent articles (6 to 11) build on this to address other scenarios, such as migration and future citizenship acquisition.

Article 6: Rights of Citizenship of Certain Persons Who Have Migrated to India from Pakistan

  • Citizenship for Migrants from Pakistan:
    • Article 6 deals with the citizenship rights of individuals who migrated to India from Pakistan before and after the partition.
    • It distinguishes between those who migrated before July 19, 1948, and those who migrated after that date.
  • Before July 19, 1948:
    • Individuals who migrated to India before July 19, 1948, are automatically granted citizenship if either parent or grandparent was born in India as defined in the Government of India Act, 1935.
  • After July 19, 1948:
    • For those who migrated after this date, citizenship is granted if they have been registered as a citizen of India after residing in India for at least six months prior to the registration.

Article 7: Rights of Citizenship of Certain Migrants to Pakistan

  • Reversing Migration:
    • Article 7 addresses the situation of individuals who migrated to Pakistan after March 1, 1947, but later returned to India.
    • These individuals are not automatically considered citizens of India.
  • Citizenship by Registration:
    • They can acquire citizenship if they return to India under a permit for resettlement or permanent return, and subsequently reside in India. They must be registered as citizens by the Government of India.
  • Exclusion Clause:
    • This article provides a mechanism to reverse the loss of citizenship for those who initially left for Pakistan but decided to return to India, acknowledging the complex migratory patterns during and after partition.

Article 8: Rights of Citizenship of Certain Persons of Indian Origin Residing Outside India

  • Citizenship for Persons of Indian Origin:
    • Article 8 provides for citizenship rights to persons of Indian origin who reside outside India.
    • Specifically, it applies to individuals who are citizens of countries other than India but have Indian ancestry.
  • Eligibility Criteria:
    • To qualify for citizenship under Article 8, a person must meet one of the following criteria:
      • Parentage: The person must be of Indian origin and either:
        • Be born in India.
        • Have one or both parents who were born in India.
      • Grandparentage: The person’s grandparents must have been born in India or in a territory that was part of India at the time of their birth.
  • Application Process:
    • Such individuals must apply for Indian citizenship under conditions specified by the Indian government.
    • The process typically involves submitting an application to the Indian authorities and fulfilling any additional requirements prescribed by the government.
  • Dual Citizenship:
    • India does not allow dual citizenship. Therefore, individuals applying for citizenship under Article 8 would need to comply with Indian laws regarding dual nationality.
  • Objective:
    • Article 8 facilitates the connection between Indian citizens living abroad and their country of origin.
    • It provides a mechanism for recognizing and granting citizenship to people of Indian origin who wish to maintain or establish a formal relationship with India.
  • Administrative Authority:
    • The central government administers the provisions of Article 8, including the procedures for granting citizenship and ensuring that applicants meet the criteria.

Article 9: Persons Voluntarily Acquiring Citizenship of a Foreign State

  • Loss of Indian Citizenship:
    • Article 9 specifies that an Indian citizen will lose their Indian citizenship if they voluntarily acquire the citizenship of another country.
    • This means that if an Indian national chooses to become a citizen of another country, they automatically cease to be an Indian citizen.
  • Automatic Disqualification:
    • The acquisition of foreign citizenship is considered an automatic disqualification for Indian citizenship.
    • There is no need for any formal procedure or declaration to lose Indian citizenship once a person acquires citizenship of another country.
  • No Dual Citizenship:
    • India does not permit dual citizenship. Therefore, a person cannot hold Indian citizenship concurrently with citizenship of another country.
    • This provision ensures that Indian nationals cannot have citizenship rights in multiple countries simultaneously.
  • Government's Role:
    • The Indian government is responsible for maintaining records of citizenship and ensuring that individuals who acquire foreign citizenship are no longer recognized as Indian citizens.
  • Implications:
    • Losing Indian citizenship under Article 9 means the individual will no longer have the rights and privileges associated with Indian citizenship, such as voting rights, and access to Indian government services.

Article 10: Continuance of the Rights of Citizenship

  • Continuance of Citizenship:
    • Article 10 provides that every person who is a citizen of India at the commencement of the Constitution (January 26, 1950) shall continue to be a citizen of India.
  • Unchanged Status:
    • This article ensures that there is no automatic loss of citizenship due to the adoption of the Constitution or changes in the legal framework of India.
  • No Retroactive Effect:
    • Article 10 confirms that the citizenship status of individuals as of the Constitution’s commencement remains unaffected by any subsequent changes or amendments to citizenship laws.
  • Legal Stability:
    • It provides legal stability and certainty, ensuring that individuals who were Indian citizens before the Constitution came into effect retain their citizenship rights.
  • Implications for Citizenship Laws:
    • While Article 10 secures the citizenship of individuals at the time of the Constitution’s commencement, subsequent laws and amendments regarding citizenship would still apply to new cases and situations.

Article 11: Power to Regulate the Right of Citizenship by Law

  • Legislative Power:
    • Article 11 grants Parliament the power to regulate and determine the rights of citizenship through legislation.
    • This includes the authority to make laws regarding the acquisition, termination, and rights associated with citizenship.
  • Scope of Legislation:
    • Parliament can create laws that define who is eligible for citizenship, how citizenship can be acquired or lost, and other related matters.
    • The article provides flexibility for future changes to citizenship laws as needed.
  • Supremacy of Law:
    • This article ensures that the determination of citizenship rights is subject to legislative action and not solely based on constitutional provisions.
