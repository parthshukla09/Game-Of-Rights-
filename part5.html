Article 52: The President of India

  1. Office of the President:
    • Article 52 establishes the office of the President of India as the head of the State. The President represents the nation and performs ceremonial functions as well as certain constitutional responsibilities.
  2. Elections and Tenure:
    • The President is elected by an Electoral College consisting of the elected members of both Houses of Parliament and the elected members of the Legislative Assemblies of States and Union territories. The term of office for the President is five years, and they are eligible for re-election.
  3. Powers and Functions:
    • Article 52 outlines the President’s role in the governance of the country, including the appointment of the Prime Minister, the approval of legislation, and the exercise of certain powers during emergencies. The President acts on the advice of the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister.
  4. Ceremonial Role:
    • In addition to constitutional duties, the President performs various ceremonial functions, including representing India on state visits and participating in national celebrations and events.
  5. Significance in Governance:
    • The President’s role is crucial in maintaining the continuity of government and ensuring that constitutional processes are followed. While the office is largely ceremonial, it plays a key role in the functioning of the Indian political system.

Article 53: Executive Power of the President

  1. Powers of the President:
    • Article 53 vests the executive power of the Union in the President of India. This power is exercised by the President on the advice of the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister.
  2. Executive Authority:
    • The President’s executive authority extends to all matters that fall under the jurisdiction of the Union Government. This includes the implementation of laws, administration of central government departments, and the appointment of officials.
  3. Emergency Powers:
    • In times of national emergency, the President has the power to assume greater control over the administration and exercise additional powers as specified by the Constitution. This includes the power to legislate by ordinance during emergencies.
  4. Delegation of Powers:
    • The President may delegate certain executive powers to subordinate authorities or officials. However, the ultimate responsibility for the delegation of power rests with the President.

Article 54: Election of the President

  1. Electoral College:
    • Article 54 outlines the process for the election of the President of India. The President is elected by an Electoral College, which consists of the elected members of both Houses of Parliament (the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha) and the elected members of the Legislative Assemblies of States and Union territories.
  2. Voting System:
    • The election is conducted using a system of proportional representation by means of a single transferable vote. Each voter in the Electoral College casts a vote, and the votes are counted in a manner that ensures representation from different states and union territories in the election process.
  3. Representation of States and Union Territories:
    • The representation of States and Union territories in the Electoral College is based on the population of each State or Union territory. This ensures that the election process is representative of the federal structure of India and balances the interests of different regions.
  4. Majority Required:
    • To be elected, a candidate must secure a majority of the votes, which are counted using a weighted system to ensure proportional representation. This ensures that the elected President has broad support across different parts of the country.
  5. Election Procedure:
    • The procedure for the President’s election is conducted under the supervision of the Election Commission of India. The process is designed to be fair and transparent, with strict guidelines to ensure the integrity of the election.

Article 55: Manner of Election of the President

  1. Principles of Election:
    • Article 55 details the manner in which the President of India is elected, ensuring that the election reflects the federal nature of the Constitution and provides a fair representation of all States and Union territories.
  2. Distribution of Votes:
    • The article specifies the formula for calculating the value of votes for members of the Electoral College. The value of votes of Members of Parliament (MPs) is determined based on the population of the States, and the value of votes of Legislative Assembly members is also calculated according to a prescribed formula to maintain proportionality.
  3. Equal Representation:
    • The system ensures that every State and Union territory is adequately represented in the election process. The value of votes is designed to balance the representation of smaller and larger States, ensuring fairness and equity in the election of the President.
  4. Counting and Allocation:
    • The article provides for the counting of votes and the allocation of votes to candidates based on a specified formula. This process is intended to reflect the principle of proportional representation and ensure that the elected President has the support of a broad segment of the population.
  5. Election Commission’s Role:
    • The Election Commission of India oversees the entire election process to ensure that it is conducted in a free and fair manner. The Commission is responsible for implementing the procedures outlined in Article 55 and ensuring compliance with constitutional requirements.

Article 56: Term of Office of President

  • Five-Year Tenure: The President of India holds office for a term of five years from the date they assume office. The five-year term is calculated from the date the President enters office, which is usually after taking the oath. However, the President does not automatically leave office at the end of the five-year term; they continue to serve until their successor has been elected and has taken office. This provision ensures there is no vacancy in the office of the President at any time.
  • Resignation Process: The President may choose to resign before the completion of the term. The resignation must be submitted in writing, addressed to the Vice President of India. This resignation is effective immediately upon being received by the Vice President. The provision for resignation allows the President to voluntarily leave office if, for any reason, they are unable or unwilling to continue in the role.
  • Impeachment and Removal: The President can be removed from office before the completion of the term through a process called impeachment. The grounds for impeachment are "violation of the Constitution." The Constitution, however, does not explicitly define what constitutes a "violation," leaving it open to interpretation. The process of impeachment is rigorous and requires a special majority in both Houses of Parliament, making it a serious and rare occurrence. This ensures that the President can only be removed under grave circumstances, safeguarding the dignity and stability of the highest constitutional office.

Article 57: Eligibility for Re-election

  • Unlimited Terms: Article 57 of the Constitution makes it clear that a person who has served as President of India is eligible for re-election. There is no limit on the number of terms a person can serve as President, unlike some other countries where the head of state is restricted to a certain number of terms. This means that theoretically, a person could be re-elected as President multiple times, provided they continue to meet the eligibility criteria and win the election. This provision ensures that experienced individuals who have previously served as President can continue to serve the nation if the electoral college chooses to re-elect them.

Article 58: Qualifications for Election as President

  • Eligibility Criteria: Article 58 specifies the qualifications required for a person to be eligible for election as President. These qualifications include:
    • Citizenship: The individual must be a citizen of India. This ensures that the highest constitutional office in the country is held by someone who is fully committed to the nation and its people.
    • Minimum Age: The individual must have attained the age of 35 years. This age requirement ensures that the candidate has a certain level of maturity and experience necessary to handle the responsibilities of the office.
    • Qualification for Lok Sabha: The individual must be qualified to be elected as a member of the Lok Sabha, the lower house of Parliament. This means that the candidate must meet all the criteria that apply to a Lok Sabha member, such as not being of unsound mind, not being an undischarged insolvent, and not holding an office of profit under the government.
    • No Office of Profit: The candidate must not hold any office of profit under the Government of India, any state government, or any local or other authority subject to the control of any of these governments. This requirement is in place to avoid conflicts of interest and to ensure the independence and impartiality of the President.

Article 59: Conditions of President's Office

  • Exclusivity of the Office: Article 59 sets out several important conditions that apply to the office of the President. These conditions ensure the independence, dignity, and impartiality of the office:
    • No Parliamentary Membership: The President cannot be a member of either House of Parliament or a State Legislature. If a sitting member of any of these bodies is elected as President, they are deemed to have vacated their seat upon entering the office of President. This ensures that the President remains above party politics and legislative biases.
    • No Other Office of Profit: The President cannot hold any other office of profit, whether under the Government of India, a state government, or any other public authority. This provision ensures that the President’s actions are not influenced by other responsibilities or financial incentives.
    • Emoluments and Allowances: The President is entitled to receive emoluments, allowances, and privileges as determined by Parliament. Importantly, these cannot be reduced during the President’s term of office. This provision is designed to protect the financial independence of the President, ensuring that they are not subject to economic pressures that could influence their decisions.

Article 60: Oath or Affirmation by the President

  • Solemn Oath: Before assuming office, the President is required to take an oath or affirmation. This is administered by the Chief Justice of India, or in their absence, the senior-most judge of the Supreme Court available. The oath is a solemn promise to:
    • Preserve, Protect, and Defend the Constitution: The President pledges to uphold the Constitution, ensuring that all actions taken are in accordance with constitutional principles and values.
    • Preserve the Law: The President also swears to preserve the law, ensuring that the rule of law is maintained and that justice is administered fairly.
    • Faithful Discharge of Duties: The oath emphasizes the importance of faithfully executing the duties of the office, underscoring the President’s responsibility to act in the best interests of the nation and its people.

Article 61: Procedure for Impeachment of the President

  • Impeachment Process: Article 61 lays down the detailed procedure for the impeachment of the President, a serious and significant process that can be initiated only on the grounds of "violation of the Constitution":
    • Initiation of Impeachment: The process can be initiated in either House of Parliament. A notice signed by at least one-fourth of the total number of members of the House proposing the impeachment is required. The notice is then delivered to the President, and a resolution to impeach the President can be moved after a 14-day notice period.
    • Special Majority Required: The resolution to impeach the President must be passed by a two-thirds majority of the total membership of the House where the impeachment process is initiated. This high threshold ensures that the decision to impeach is taken with broad consensus, reflecting the gravity of the action.
    • Investigation by the Other House: After the resolution is passed by one House, the other House investigates the charges. During this investigation, the President has the right to appear and be represented to defend themselves against the charges.
    • Final Decision: If the second House also passes the impeachment resolution by a two-thirds majority of its total membership, the President stands removed from office. This two-stage process with rigorous requirements ensures that impeachment is not undertaken lightly and only in cases of serious constitutional violations.

Article 62: Time of Holding Election to Fill Vacancy in the Office of President

  • Timely Election: Article 62 ensures that the office of the President is never left vacant for an extended period:
    • Election Before Term Expiry: The election to fill a vacancy caused by the expiration of the President’s term must be completed before the term ends. This provision ensures a seamless transition from one President to the next, maintaining continuity in the highest office.
    • Vacancy Due to Other Causes: In the event of a vacancy due to death, resignation, impeachment, or any other reason, an election must be held within six months. This ensures that the office of the President is filled promptly, even in unforeseen circumstances.
    • Vice President as Acting President: Until the new President is elected and takes office, the Vice President acts as President. This provision ensures that the functions of the office of the President are not interrupted, even temporarily.

Article 63: The Vice-President of India

  • Creation of the Office: Article 63 mandates the establishment of the office of the Vice President of India. The Vice President is the second-highest constitutional office in the country, ranking just below the President.
  • Role and Responsibilities: The Vice President serves as the ex officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha (Council of States). In this capacity, the Vice President presides over the sessions of the Rajya Sabha and ensures the smooth conduct of its business. This dual role of the Vice President as both a part of the executive branch and the legislative branch underscores the unique nature of the office.
  • Succession to the Presidency: In the event of a vacancy in the office of the President, the Vice President acts as President until a new President is elected. This makes the Vice President an essential figure in ensuring continuity in the executive branch of government.

Article 64: The Vice-President to be Ex-Officio Chairman of the Council of States

  • Chairman of the Rajya Sabha: Article 64 specifically designates the Vice President as the ex officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha. In this role, the Vice President:
    • Presiding Over Sessions: The Vice President presides over the sessions of the Rajya Sabha, ensuring that debates are conducted in an orderly manner and that parliamentary procedures are followed.
    • Casting Vote: The Vice President does not vote in the Rajya Sabha, except in the case of a tie, where they exercise a casting vote. This ensures that the Vice President’s role is neutral and impartial.
    • Absence as Chairman: When the Vice President is acting as President, they do not perform the duties of the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha. During such times, the Deputy Chairman or another member of the Rajya Sabha presides over its sessions.

Article 65: The Vice-President to Act as President or to Discharge His Functions During Casual Vacancies

  • Acting as President: Article 65 outlines the role of the Vice President in acting as President during any period when the office of the President is vacant:
    • Casual Vacancies: If the office of the President becomes vacant due to death, resignation, impeachment, or any other reason, the Vice President steps in as the Acting President. This ensures that there is no power vacuum in the executive branch of the government.
    • Temporary Absences: If the President is temporarily unable to discharge their duties due to illness, travel, or other reasons, the Vice President takes over the responsibilities. This provision ensures that the functions of the President are carried out without interruption.
    • Duration of Acting Role: The Vice President continues to act as President until a new President is elected and assumes office, or until the President returns and resumes their duties. This continuity is crucial for the stability of the executive branch.

Article 66: Election of Vice-President

  • Electoral College:
    • Constitution of Electoral College: The Vice President of India is elected by an electoral college consisting of members of both Houses of Parliament, namely the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha. Unlike the President's election, which involves members of the State Legislative Assemblies, the Vice President's election is confined to the central legislature.
    • Proportional Representation: The election is held using the system of proportional representation by means of a single transferable vote, ensuring that the elected candidate represents the broadest possible support within the Parliament. This system allows members to rank candidates in order of preference, ensuring that even if no candidate receives an outright majority, the votes can be transferred based on preferences until one candidate emerges as the winner.
  • Eligibility Criteria:
    • Age and Citizenship: The candidate for the Vice Presidency must be a citizen of India and must have completed 35 years of age. This requirement ensures that the candidate possesses the maturity and experience needed for the role.
    • Qualification for Rajya Sabha Membership: The candidate must be qualified for election as a member of the Rajya Sabha, the Council of States. This implies that the candidate should not hold any office of profit under the Government of India or any State Government, which could lead to a conflict of interest.
  • No Membership in Parliament or State Legislature:
    • Vacating Office: If a sitting member of Parliament or a State Legislature is elected as Vice President, they are required to vacate their seat in the legislature. This ensures the Vice President’s impartiality and neutrality, preventing any conflict between legislative duties and the responsibilities of the Vice Presidency.

Article 67: Term of Office of Vice-President

  • Five-Year Term: The Vice President holds office for a term of five years from the date they enter upon their office. This fixed term provides stability and continuity to the office.
  • Continuance Beyond Term: Even after the completion of the five-year term, the Vice President continues to hold office until their successor takes over. This provision ensures that there is no vacancy in the office at any time.
  • Resignation and Removal: The Vice President may resign from office by submitting a resignation letter to the President. Additionally, the Vice President can be removed from office by a resolution passed by a majority of all the members of the Rajya Sabha and agreed to by the Lok Sabha. This process ensures that the Vice President can be held accountable, but it also provides protection against arbitrary removal.

Article 68: Time of Holding Election to Fill Vacancy in the Office of Vice-President

  • Election Timing:
    • Before Term Expiry: An election to fill a vacancy in the office of the Vice President caused by the expiration of the term must be completed before the term ends. This ensures a smooth transition and continuity in the office.
    • Filling Casual Vacancies: In the event of a vacancy due to the death, resignation, removal, or otherwise, the election must be held as soon as possible after the vacancy occurs. This prompt action ensures that the office does not remain vacant for an extended period, which could disrupt the functioning of the Rajya Sabha and the succession process for the Presidency.
    • Acting Role: If the office of the Vice President becomes vacant, the Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha or the senior-most member of the Rajya Sabha temporarily presides over its sessions until a new Vice President is elected. This provision ensures that the Rajya Sabha continues to function without interruption.

Article 69: Oath or Affirmation by the Vice-President

  • Oath of Office:
    • Administering the Oath: Before entering the office, the Vice President is required to take an oath or affirmation. This oath is administered by the President of India or, in their absence, by a person appointed by the President.
    • Content of the Oath:
      • Bear True Faith and Allegiance: The Vice President pledges to bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India, ensuring that their actions and decisions are guided by constitutional principles.
      • Faithfully Discharge Duties: The Vice President promises to faithfully discharge the duties of their office without fear or favor, ensuring impartiality in their role as Chairman of the Rajya Sabha and as Acting President when required.
      • Preserve and Protect the Constitution: The oath underscores the Vice President’s responsibility to preserve, protect, and defend the Constitution, aligning with the similar commitment made by the President.

Article 70: Discharge of President’s Functions in Other Contingencies

  • Provision for Extraordinary Situations:
    • Unforeseen Circumstances: Article 70 provides flexibility to address extraordinary situations where the office of both the President and the Vice President may be vacant simultaneously or if both are unable to discharge their functions. While such situations are extremely rare, this provision ensures that the functioning of the executive branch is not paralyzed.
    • Parliamentary Authority: The Constitution empowers Parliament to make provisions for the discharge of the President’s functions in such contingencies. This allows Parliament to enact laws or pass resolutions that designate an appropriate authority or process to handle the responsibilities of the President temporarily.
    • Continuity of Governance: This article ensures that there is always a clear line of succession or a mechanism in place to discharge the functions of the President, thus maintaining the stability and continuity of governance even in the most extraordinary circumstances.

Article 71: Matters Relating to, or Connected with, the Election of a President or Vice-President

  • Jurisdiction of the Supreme Court:
    • Exclusive Jurisdiction: Article 71 grants the Supreme Court of India the exclusive jurisdiction to resolve disputes related to the election of the President and the Vice President. This means that any doubts or disputes arising in connection with the election of these high offices are decided solely by the Supreme Court, ensuring impartiality and legal finality.
    • Election Disputes: Disputes can arise on various grounds, such as the validity of nominations, the conduct of the election, or the counting of votes. The Supreme Court's judgment on these matters is final and binding, which underscores the integrity of the electoral process for the highest offices in the country.
  • Impact of Supreme Court Decision:
    • Effect of Decision: If the Supreme Court determines that the election of the President or Vice President is void, the election is declared invalid, and a fresh election must be conducted. This provision ensures that any malpractice or violation of the law during the election process is corrected.
    • Continuity in Office: The Constitution ensures that the functions of the President or Vice President continue to be carried out until the Supreme Court gives its decision. This prevents any disruption in the functioning of the executive branch of the government.
  • Parliamentary Legislation:
    • Authority to Regulate: Parliament has the authority to regulate the manner in which disputes related to the election of the President or Vice President are presented to the Supreme Court. This includes the procedure for filing petitions and the conditions under which they can be entertained, ensuring a structured and fair process.

Article 72: Power of President to Grant Pardons, Etc., and to Suspend, Remit, or Commute Sentences in Certain Cases

  • Presidential Clemency Powers:
    • Grant of Pardons: The President of India has the constitutional power to grant pardons to individuals convicted of offenses, particularly in cases where the punishment is a death sentence. A pardon fully absolves the individual from the legal consequences of the crime and their sentence, effectively freeing them from the punishment.
    • Remission of Punishment: The President can reduce the duration of a sentence without changing its nature, a power known as remission. For instance, a ten-year sentence might be reduced to five years, reflecting a degree of mercy while still upholding the conviction.
    • Commutation of Sentence: The President can also commute, or change, the nature of the sentence to one that is less severe. For example, a death sentence might be commuted to life imprisonment, or a rigorous imprisonment sentence might be commuted to simple imprisonment.
    • Suspension of Sentence: The President may suspend the execution of a sentence, which temporarily halts the enforcement of the punishment while the suspension is in effect. This power can be used to delay the execution of a sentence, pending a decision on a pardon or commutation.
  • Scope of Clemency Powers:
    • Military Court-Martials: The President’s clemency powers extend to cases tried by military courts, or court-martials, providing a higher level of review and mercy in the military justice system.
    • Scope Across Jurisdictions: These powers can be exercised in cases involving laws made by the Union or relating to matters under the Union’s executive power, covering a wide range of federal and criminal matters.

Article 73: Extent of Executive Power of the Union

  • Executive Power of the Union:
    • Scope of Union Authority: Article 73 defines the extent of the executive power of the Union, which is co-extensive with the legislative power of Parliament. This means that the Union Executive, led by the President, can exercise authority over all subjects on which Parliament can legislate, including matters listed in the Union List and Concurrent List.
    • Power over International Affairs: The Union Executive has exclusive authority over foreign affairs, treaties, and other international matters. This centralization ensures a cohesive and consistent national foreign policy and the ability to engage in international relations on behalf of India.
  • Limitations:
    • State Matters: The executive power of the Union does not extend to subjects on which the State Legislature has exclusive legislative power, ensuring that states retain their autonomy over certain matters. However, the Union may intervene in state matters under certain circumstances, such as in the event of a national emergency or a breakdown of constitutional machinery in a state.

Article 74: Council of Ministers to Aid and Advise the President

  • Advisory Role of the Council of Ministers:
    • Binding Advice: Article 74 establishes that the President of India is required to act in accordance with the advice given by the Council of Ministers, headed by the Prime Minister. This provision underscores the democratic principle that the executive power is exercised not by the President alone, but on the basis of collective advice from the elected government.
    • Council’s Composition: The Council of Ministers includes the Prime Minister and other ministers who are appointed by the President on the Prime Minister’s advice. The composition reflects the ruling party or coalition’s majority in the Lok Sabha, ensuring that the executive is representative of the elected legislative majority.
  • Role of the President:
    • The President has the right to seek information on any matter and can require the Council to reconsider its advice. However, if the Council of Ministers reiterates its advice after reconsideration, the President is obligated to act according to that advice.

Article 75: Other Provisions as to Ministers

  • Appointment and Tenure of Ministers:
    • Prime Minister’s Appointment: The President appoints the Prime Minister, who is typically the leader of the majority party or coalition in the Lok Sabha. This ensures that the head of the executive branch has the support of the majority in the lower house of Parliament.
    • Council of Ministers: The President appoints other ministers on the advice of the Prime Minister. The Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha, meaning that they must maintain the confidence of the majority in the lower house to remain in office.
  • Salaries and Allowances:
    • Parliamentary Determination: The salaries and allowances of the Prime Minister and other ministers are determined by Parliament. This ensures that remuneration is subject to legislative control and is transparent and accountable.
  • Disqualification:
    • Ministerial Qualifications: To ensure accountability, Article 75 specifies that a minister must be a member of either House of Parliament. If a minister is not a member at the time of their appointment, they must secure membership within six months, or they must resign from the Council of Ministers.

Article 76: Attorney-General for India

  • Role of the Attorney-General:
    • Chief Legal Advisor: The Attorney-General for India is the chief legal advisor to the Government of India and represents the Union in legal matters. Appointed by the President, the Attorney-General must be qualified to be appointed as a judge of the Supreme Court, reflecting the importance of the position.
    • Duties and Functions: The Attorney-General’s duties include giving legal advice to the government, representing the Union in the Supreme Court and other courts, and performing any legal duties assigned by the President. This role is critical in ensuring that the government’s actions and policies are legally sound.
  • Participation in Parliament:
    • Right to Speak: The Attorney-General has the right to speak and take part in the proceedings of both Houses of Parliament and any parliamentary committee, but without the right to vote. This provision ensures that the government has access to expert legal opinions during legislative debates.
  • Independence:
    • No Fixed Tenure: The Attorney-General holds office at the pleasure of the President, meaning there is no fixed tenure. This arrangement allows for flexibility but also underscores the importance of maintaining the confidence of the government.

Article 77: Conduct of Business of the Government of India

  • Executive Actions:
    • In the Name of the President: All executive actions of the Government of India must be expressed to be taken in the name of the President. This provision ensures that all official acts, orders, and decisions are carried out under the authority of the President, symbolizing the unity of the executive branch.
    • Authentication: The manner in which orders and other instruments made and executed in the name of the President are authenticated is determined by rules made by the President. This provides a standardized process for ensuring the legality and authenticity of government actions.
  • Allocation of Business:
    • President’s Power to Allocate: The President has the power to make rules for the more convenient transaction of the government’s business and for the allocation of such business among the ministers. This ensures an organized and efficient administration, with clear responsibilities assigned to each minister.

Article 78: Duties of Prime Minister as Respecting the Furnishing of Information to the President, Etc.

  • Prime Minister’s Responsibilities:
    • Communication with the President: The Prime Minister is responsible for keeping the President informed about all decisions of the Council of Ministers relating to the administration of the Union and proposals for legislation. This ensures that the President is kept up-to-date on government affairs, even though they must act on the Council’s advice.
    • Seeking Presidential Direction: The Prime Minister must also furnish any information relating to the Union’s administration and legislative proposals that the President may request. This provision ensures a flow of information between the head of government and the head of state.
    • Submission of Council’s Decisions for Reconsideration: The Prime Minister is obligated to submit for the President’s reconsideration any decision taken by a minister but not yet considered by the Council, if so requested by the President. This allows the President to exercise a degree of oversight and suggest reconsideration of certain decisions.

Article 79: Constitution of Parliament

  • Bicameral Legislature:
    • Two Houses: Article 79 establishes that the Parliament of India consists of the President and two Houses: the Council of States (Rajya Sabha) and the House of the People (Lok Sabha). This bicameral structure is designed to balance the representation of the states and the people, with the Rajya Sabha representing states and the Lok Sabha representing the population at large.
    • Role of the President: The President of India is an integral part of Parliament, playing a key role in the legislative process by summoning, proroguing, and dissolving the Lok Sabha, as well as giving assent to bills passed by both Houses.

Article 80: Composition of the Council of States (Rajya Sabha)

  • Membership Structure:
    • Elected and Nominated Members: The Rajya Sabha consists of a maximum of 250 members, of which 238 are representatives of the states and Union territories, elected by the elected members of the State Legislative Assemblies and by members of the Electoral college for Union territories using a system of proportional representation. The remaining 12 members are nominated by the President for their expertise in fields such as literature, science, art, and social service.
    • Representation of States and Union Territories: The number of representatives from each state in the Rajya Sabha is proportional to the state’s population, ensuring that more populous states have greater representation. This structure balances the representation of states within the federal system.
  • Term of Office:
    • Rotational Membership: Members of the Rajya Sabha are elected for a term of six years, with one-third of the members retiring every two years. This system ensures continuity within the House, while also allowing for regular infusion of new members and ideas.
    • Permanent Body: Unlike the Lok Sabha, the Rajya Sabha is not subject to dissolution, making it a permanent body. This stability allows the Rajya Sabha to play a key role in reviewing and amending legislation, providing a check on the more politically dynamic Lok Sabha.

Article 81: Composition of the House of the People (Lok Sabha)

  • Structure and Composition:
    • Maximum Membership: Article 81 specifies the composition of the Lok Sabha, the lower house of Parliament, with a maximum strength initially set at 552 members. This includes up to 530 representatives from the states, up to 20 from the Union territories, and up to 2 members of the Anglo-Indian community nominated by the President, if the community is not adequately represented.
    • Representation Based on Population: The allocation of seats to the states in the Lok Sabha is based on their respective populations. The Constitution ensures proportional representation, where each state’s population determines the number of seats allocated to it, balancing the representation of different regions within the Union.
  • Constituency Delimitation:
    • Uniformity of Representation: The Constitution mandates that the ratio between the population of each constituency and the number of seats allocated must be as uniform as possible. This is to ensure fairness in representation, where each elected member represents a roughly equal number of people.
    • Delimitation Commission: The actual division of the state into constituencies is carried out by a Delimitation Commission, an independent body established to ensure an impartial and transparent process. The Commission periodically reviews and redraws constituency boundaries to reflect changes in population and ensure balanced representation.

Article 82: Readjustment After Each Census

  • Periodic Readjustment:
    • Census-Based Reallocation: Article 82 provides for the reallocation of seats in the Lok Sabha and the readjustment of the division of states into constituencies after each decennial census. This ensures that representation in the House of the People accurately reflects demographic changes, maintaining the principle of proportional representation.
    • Parliamentary Authority: The responsibility for carrying out these adjustments lies with Parliament, which can pass laws to effect changes based on the census data. This provision ensures that the political structure evolves in response to population shifts.
  • Delimitation Suspension:
    • Suspension Periods: Historically, the Constitution has been amended to suspend the delimitation process for specific periods. For example, the 42nd Amendment in 1976 froze the allocation of seats and the division of constituencies until the results of the 2001 census, aiming to encourage population control measures. The freeze was extended until 2026 by the 84th Amendment in 2001, emphasizing the balance between representation and population growth management.

Article 83: Duration of Houses of Parliament

  • Term of the Lok Sabha:
    • Five-Year Term: The Lok Sabha, the House of the People, is elected for a term of five years. However, this term can be extended during a period of national emergency by an act of Parliament, by one year at a time, but not beyond six months after the emergency has ended. This provision ensures democratic continuity while allowing flexibility in times of crisis.
    • Dissolution by the President: The President of India has the power to dissolve the Lok Sabha before the completion of its term, usually on the advice of the Prime Minister. This dissolution typically leads to general elections, allowing the electorate to choose a new government.
  • Rajya Sabha’s Continuity:
    • Permanent Body: Unlike the Lok Sabha, the Rajya Sabha, or the Council of States, is a permanent body that is not subject to dissolution. One-third of its members retire every two years, ensuring continuity and stability within the upper house. This structure allows the Rajya Sabha to play a crucial role in reviewing and refining legislation without the disruptions caused by regular elections.

Article 84: Qualification for Membership of Parliament

  • Eligibility Criteria:
    • Citizenship: To be eligible for membership in either the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha, a person must be a citizen of India. This requirement ensures that only those who are legally part of the Indian polity can represent the people in Parliament.
    • Minimum Age: For the Lok Sabha, a candidate must be at least 25 years old, while for the Rajya Sabha, the minimum age is 30 years. This age criterion is meant to ensure that members have attained a certain level of maturity and experience before taking on the responsibilities of lawmaking.
    • Other Qualifications: The Constitution also requires that a candidate possess other qualifications as may be prescribed by law. Parliament has the authority to set these additional qualifications, such as those related to moral character, educational background, or absence of criminal records.

Article 84: Qualification for Membership of Parliament

  • Eligibility Criteria:
    • Citizenship: To be eligible for membership in either the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha, a person must be a citizen of India. This requirement ensures that only those who are legally part of the Indian polity can represent the people in Parliament.
    • Minimum Age: For the Lok Sabha, a candidate must be at least 25 years old, while for the Rajya Sabha, the minimum age is 30 years. This age criterion is meant to ensure that members have attained a certain level of maturity and experience before taking on the responsibilities of lawmaking.
    • Other Qualifications: The Constitution also requires that a candidate possess other qualifications as may be prescribed by law. Parliament has the authority to set these additional qualifications, such as those related to moral character, educational background, or absence of criminal records.

Article 85: Sessions of Parliament, Prorogation, and Dissolution

  • Parliamentary Sessions:
    • Annual Sessions: Article 85 mandates that Parliament must meet at least twice a year, with no more than six months between its last sitting in one session and the first sitting in the next. This ensures that Parliament convenes regularly to perform its legislative functions and address national issues.
    • Summoning by the President: The President of India has the power to summon each House of Parliament to meet at the beginning of each session. The President also has the authority to prorogue (end) a session of Parliament, marking the completion of its business for that period.
  • Dissolution of Lok Sabha:
    • Presidential Power: The President, on the advice of the Prime Minister, can dissolve the Lok Sabha, thereby terminating its existence and leading to general elections. Dissolution is usually recommended when the government loses its majority in the House or when it seeks a fresh mandate from the electorate.

Article 86: Right of President to Address and Send Messages to Houses

  • Presidential Address:
    • Right to Address Parliament: Article 86 grants the President the right to address either House of Parliament or both Houses assembled together. This power is most commonly exercised at the beginning of the first session after a general election and at the start of the first session each year. The Presidential address outlines the government’s policies and legislative agenda for the upcoming session, setting the tone for parliamentary debates.
    • Message to Parliament: The President can also send messages to either House of Parliament with respect to a bill pending in the House or any other matter. When such a message is received, the House is required to consider it with all due urgency, reflecting the President’s role as the ceremonial head of state and the ultimate guardian of the Constitution.

Article 87: Special Address by the President

  • Annual and Special Addresses:
    • Inaugural Address: Article 87 mandates that the President shall address both Houses of Parliament assembled together at the commencement of the first session after each general election and at the first session of each year. This address serves to inform Parliament about the policies of the government, its achievements, and its agenda for the upcoming year. It also provides an opportunity for the government to outline its legislative priorities.
    • Discussion of Address: Following the President’s address, a motion of thanks is moved in both Houses, leading to a general discussion. This allows members of Parliament to debate the government’s policies and express their opinions, contributing to a broader understanding of the government’s direction.

Article 88: Rights of Ministers and Attorney-General as Respects Houses

  • Participation Rights:
    • Ministers in Parliament: Article 88 provides that every minister has the right to speak in, and take part in the proceedings of, either House of Parliament and any joint sitting of the Houses, as well as any committee of Parliament of which they may be a member. This right is irrespective of whether the minister is a member of the particular House or not. It ensures that ministers can actively participate in debates and discussions, providing explanations, clarifications, and information related to their ministries.
    • Attorney-General’s Role: Similarly, the Attorney-General for India, the chief legal advisor to the government, has the right to take part in the proceedings of either House or any parliamentary committee of which they may be a member, without the right to vote. This ensures that legal advice is readily available during legislative deliberations, aiding in the formulation of legally sound policies and laws.

Article 89: The Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Council of States (Rajya Sabha)

  • Presiding Officers:
    • Chairman of Rajya Sabha: The Vice President of India is ex-officio the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha, responsible for presiding over its sessions. The Chairman’s role is crucial for maintaining order during debates, deciding on points of order, and ensuring the smooth conduct of proceedings. The Chairman, however, does not have a vote in the Rajya Sabha except in the case of a tie, where they exercise a casting vote to break the deadlock.
    • Deputy Chairman: The Rajya Sabha elects a Deputy Chairman from among its members, who takes on the duties of the Chairman in their absence. The Deputy Chairman plays a key role in assisting the Chairman and ensuring that the House’s business is conducted efficiently and impartially.
  • Removal Process:
    • Procedure for Removal: The Chairman or the Deputy Chairman can be removed from office by a resolution passed by a majority of all the then members of the Council of States. However, a notice of at least 14 days must be given before such a resolution can be moved, ensuring due process and preventing arbitrary removal.

Article 90: Vacation and Resignation of, and Removal from, the Office of Deputy Chairman

  • Deputy Chairman’s Office:
    • Vacation of Office: Article 90 outlines the conditions under which the Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha vacates their office. This occurs if they cease to be a member of the Rajya Sabha, if they resign by writing to the Chairman, or if they are removed by a resolution passed by a majority of the members of the Rajya Sabha.
    • Resignation Process: The Deputy Chairman may resign from their position by submitting their resignation in writing to the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha. This ensures a formal and clear process for stepping down from the role.
    • Removal: The procedure for removal is similar to that of the Chairman, requiring a resolution passed by a majority of the members of the Rajya Sabha, with at least 14 days’ notice. This safeguards the position and ensures that removal is considered carefully and with due deliberation.

Article 91: Power of the Deputy Chairman or other person to perform the duties of the Chairman

  • Provision for Absence: This article outlines that in situations where the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha (who is also the Vice President of India) is absent, or the office of the Chairman is vacant for any reason, the Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha is empowered to perform all duties and functions of the Chairman. This ensures that there is no disruption in the functioning of the Rajya Sabha due to the Chairman's absence.
  • Authorization of Other Members: If both the Chairman and Deputy Chairman are unavailable, the President of India has the authority to authorize another member of the Rajya Sabha to fulfill these duties temporarily. This provision allows the legislative body to continue its operations smoothly without interruptions.
  • Continuity of Operations: The purpose of this article is to maintain the continuity of legislative work in the Rajya Sabha, ensuring that there is always someone available to preside over sessions, make necessary decisions, and oversee the proceedings, regardless of any unforeseen circumstances that might render the Chairman unavailable.

Article 92: The Chairman or Deputy Chairman not to preside while a resolution for his removal is under consideration

  • Impartiality in Removal Process: This article specifies that when the Rajya Sabha is considering a resolution to remove either the Chairman or the Deputy Chairman from their office, the individual in question cannot preside over the meeting during the consideration of such a resolution. This provision is critical to ensure impartiality and fairness in the removal process, as it prevents the person who is the subject of the resolution from influencing the proceedings.
  • Alternative Presiding Arrangements: During this period, the duties of presiding over the Rajya Sabha session are transferred to another member, as provided by Article 91. This ensures that the legislative body continues to function without bias or disruption while the resolution is being debated and voted upon.
  • Ensuring Fairness: This safeguard is important to maintain the integrity of the parliamentary process, particularly in cases where there might be significant reasons for the removal of the presiding officer. By removing any potential conflict of interest, the article ensures that the Rajya Sabha can make decisions regarding the removal in a fair and unbiased manner.

Article 93: The Speaker and Deputy Speaker of the House of the People

  • Election of Speaker and Deputy Speaker: This article mandates that the Lok Sabha (House of the People) must elect two members from among its own to serve as the Speaker and Deputy Speaker. The election of these officers is one of the first actions taken after the formation of a new Lok Sabha following general elections. Their election ensures that the Lok Sabha has leadership and direction in its proceedings.
  • Roles and Responsibilities of the Speaker: The Speaker plays a critical role in the functioning of the Lok Sabha. As the presiding officer, the Speaker is responsible for maintaining order during debates, ensuring that all members have the opportunity to participate, and that the rules and procedures of the House are followed. The Speaker also represents the House in its relations with the President of India and other authorities and has a significant role in the legislative process, including the ability to decide on questions of disqualification of members under the Anti-Defection Law.
  • Deputy Speaker’s Role: The Deputy Speaker assists the Speaker in their duties and takes over the role of presiding officer in the Speaker's absence. This ensures that there is always a designated leader available to oversee the proceedings of the Lok Sabha. The presence of both a Speaker and a Deputy Speaker allows for continuity and stability in the leadership of the House.

Article 94: Vacation and resignation of, and removal from, the offices of Speaker and Deputy Speaker

  • Conditions for Vacating Office: Article 94 outlines several conditions under which the Speaker or Deputy Speaker must vacate their office. One key condition is that they must step down if they cease to be a member of the Lok Sabha, which can occur if they lose their seat in an election, resign from the Lok Sabha, or are disqualified as a member for any reason.
  • Resignation Process: The article also provides that the Speaker or Deputy Speaker can resign from their position at any time by submitting a written resignation to the Deputy Speaker or Speaker, respectively. This formal procedure ensures a clear and orderly transition of responsibilities.
  • Removal by Resolution: Additionally, the Lok Sabha has the power to remove the Speaker or Deputy Speaker from office through a resolution passed by a majority of all the then-members of the House. This requires a notice of at least 14 days before the resolution is moved, allowing time for proper consideration and debate. The removal process is a crucial check on the authority of these officers, ensuring they can be held accountable by the members of the Lok Sabha.

Article 95: Power of the Deputy Speaker or other person to perform the duties of the office of, or to act as, Speaker

  • Deputy Speaker Acting as Speaker: In the event that the Speaker is unable to perform their duties due to absence, illness, or any other reason, the Deputy Speaker assumes the role of the Speaker. This ensures that the legislative process in the Lok Sabha is not interrupted and continues smoothly, with a designated leader always in place to preside over the House.
  • Provision for Both Being Absent: If both the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker are absent, the rules of the Lok Sabha or, if necessary, a person appointed by the President of India will take over the duties. This provision is important for maintaining the continuity of the House’s operations, ensuring that a session can always proceed with proper oversight and management.
  • Flexibility and Continuity: Article 95 provides the necessary flexibility to adapt to various situations that may arise due to the absence of key leaders in the Lok Sabha. It ensures that there is always someone available to carry out the essential functions of the Speaker, thereby safeguarding the procedural integrity of the House.

Article 96: The Speaker or the Deputy Speaker not to preside while a resolution for his removal from office is under consideration

  • Exclusion from Presiding: Similar to the provisions in Article 92 for the Rajya Sabha, this article states that if a resolution for the removal of the Speaker or Deputy Speaker is being considered by the Lok Sabha, the individual facing removal cannot preside over the session. This ensures that the process is conducted fairly, without any undue influence from the person whose removal is under consideration.
  • Alternative Presiding Arrangements: In such cases, the duties of presiding over the session are transferred to another member, as provided by Article 95. This arrangement maintains the neutrality of the House during such critical proceedings, ensuring that all decisions are made impartially.
  • Ensuring Integrity: This article plays a crucial role in maintaining the integrity of the removal process by preventing any potential conflict of interest. It ensures that the proceedings are carried out in a manner that upholds the democratic principles of transparency and fairness within the parliamentary system.

Article 97: Salaries and allowances of the Chairman and Deputy Chairman and the Speaker and Deputy Speaker

  • Parliament’s Authority on Salaries: Article 97 establishes that the salaries and allowances of the Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha, as well as the Speaker and Deputy Speaker of the Lok Sabha, are determined by Parliament. This provision grants the legislative body the authority to set appropriate remuneration for these key officials based on their significant responsibilities and duties.
  • Charge on Consolidated Fund of India: The article further specifies that these salaries and allowances are charged upon the Consolidated Fund of India, meaning that they are not subject to the annual vote of Parliament. This ensures financial independence for these officers, preventing any potential influence or pressure from the executive branch or other members of Parliament.
  • Ensuring Adequate Compensation: By securing their remuneration through this article, the Constitution ensures that these officials are adequately compensated for their services, allowing them to perform their duties with the independence and impartiality required by their positions.

Article 98: Secretariat of Parliament

  • Separate Secretariats for Each House: Article 98 mandates that each House of Parliament, the Rajya Sabha and the Lok Sabha, must have its own secretariat staffed by individuals recruited specifically for that House. These secretariats are responsible for managing the day-to-day administrative and procedural operations, including organizing sessions, maintaining records, and providing assistance to members.
  • Regulation by Parliament: The recruitment, conditions of service, and other aspects related to the secretarial staff are regulated by Parliament, ensuring that both Houses have the necessary administrative support to function efficiently. This regulation allows Parliament to set standards and procedures that align with the unique needs of each House.
  • Provision for Common Services: The article also allows for the possibility of sharing common services between the two Houses, such as a joint library or research service, provided there is mutual agreement. This flexibility enables the efficient use of resources while maintaining the distinct identity and functioning of each House’s secretariat.

Article 99: Oath or affirmation by members

  • Mandatory Oath or Affirmation: Article 99 requires that every member of Parliament, whether in the Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha, must take an oath or make an affirmation before they can take their seat in the House. This is a formal process that signifies the member’s commitment to uphold the Constitution of India, serve the nation faithfully, and discharge their duties with integrity.
  • Administration of the Oath: The oath or affirmation is administered by the President of India or a person appointed by the President for this purpose. The specific wording of the oath or affirmation is prescribed in the Third Schedule of the Constitution, reflecting the solemn nature of the responsibilities that come with being a member of Parliament.
  • Significance of the Oath: Taking this oath or affirmation is not just a procedural formality but an important act that underscores the member’s allegiance to the Constitution and their commitment to their parliamentary duties.

Article 100: Voting in Houses, Power of Houses to Act Notwithstanding Vacancies, and Quorum

  • Voting Procedure:
    • Decision-Making: Article 100 mandates that any decision or resolution on matters discussed in either House of Parliament (the Rajya Sabha or the Lok Sabha) must be determined by a majority of votes of the members who are present and actively voting. This ensures that decisions are made based on the collective will of the members present during a session.
    • Casting Vote: In case of a tie, where votes are evenly split, the presiding officer (Speaker in the Lok Sabha or Chairman in the Rajya Sabha) is given a casting vote. This is an additional, decisive vote used to break the tie and determine the outcome of the matter under consideration. The casting vote is exercised at the discretion of the presiding officer, often used to maintain the status quo or to decide in favor of a particular direction if it aligns with parliamentary convention or established practice.
  • Acting Despite Vacancies:
    • Continuity of Operations: The article stipulates that both Houses of Parliament can continue to conduct their business and act on legislative matters even if there are vacancies in their membership. This ensures that legislative functions are not disrupted or stalled due to unfilled seats or vacancies that may arise for various reasons, such as resignations, deaths, or disqualifications of members.
    • Efficient Functioning: This provision supports the efficient functioning of Parliament by allowing it to carry out its duties and pass legislation without being hindered by the absence of a full complement of members. It emphasizes the resilience and continuity of parliamentary processes despite potential disruptions.
  • Quorum Requirements:
    • Definition of Quorum: Article 100 defines the quorum for each House, which is the minimum number of members required to be present for the House to conduct its business validly. For the Lok Sabha, the quorum is one-tenth of the total number of its members, while for the Rajya Sabha, it is one-tenth of its members.
    • Maintenance of Quorum: If the number of members present falls below the quorum during a session, the presiding officer may take measures such as adjourning the House or suspending proceedings until the quorum is restored. This provision ensures that decisions and proceedings of the House are conducted with the requisite minimum level of representation, thereby upholding the legitimacy and validity of parliamentary actions.
